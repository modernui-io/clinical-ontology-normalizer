\paragraph{Why vanilla RAG hurts.}
Clinical notes contain dense negated, uncertain, and historical findings.
When vanilla RAG retrieves ``patient denies diabetes,'' the word ``diabetes'' enters context without the epistemic frame distinguishing absence from presence, actively misleading the model.
The C2~$<$~C1 finding ($-9.0\pp$, Table~\ref{tab:clinicalbench_ablation}) is consistent with GraphRAG-Bench~\cite{graphragbench2026}, which reports that GraphRAG underperforms when graph structure fails to encode task-relevant semantics---here, assertion status.

\paragraph{When KG context matters.}
The complexity-dependent SliceBench effect (Tier~C: $+5.0\pp$ vs.\ Tier~A: $+0.6\pp$) suggests structured epistemic context is most valuable when: (a)~document volume overwhelms chunk-level retrieval, (b)~questions require cross-encounter synthesis where the same concept appears with different assertion states, and (c)~assertion status evolves across encounters.
For simple patients with few notes, the LLM tracks assertion context within retrieved chunks and the KG adds little.
C4g's intent-aware routing provides direct evidence for this mechanism: the ``change'' category---which was near-zero across all prior conditions including C4---jumps to 60\% under C4g ($+60\pp$ vs.\ C1), confirming that \emph{targeted graph traversal strategy}, not merely graph structure, is the key factor.
By routing change questions to cross-admission edge partitioning and current-state questions to recency-weighted retrieval, C4g demonstrates that matching retrieval mode to question intent unlocks gains that uniform retrieval cannot.

\paragraph{Model-strength interaction.}
The cross-model results (Table~\ref{tab:crossmodel}) show that epistemic KG-RAG's benefit is inversely related to baseline model capability.
Under the uniform C4 condition, MedGemma~27B showed no aggregate change ($+0\pp$) and Opus showed a small regression ($-2.2\pp$).
However, C4g reveals a \emph{category-specific} interaction: under intent-aware routing, MedGemma gains $+60\pp$ on change and $+57\pp$ on family history while losing ground where its C1 baseline is already adequate.
Under C4, Opus gains on assertion-dependent categories (negation $+2.7\pp$, duration $+10.0\pp$, uncertainty $+7.5\pp$) but regresses on conditional ($-15.0\pp$) and family history ($-10.0\pp$).
This reveals a design trade-off: structured context is transformative for cross-admission reasoning but can override correct answers where parametric knowledge suffices.
Model-adaptive evidence presentation (\eg confidence-gated injection) is a promising direction.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Sample size is modest: SliceBench covers 6 patients (144 questions) and ClinicalBench covers 45 patients (400 questions), both from a single site (MIMIC-IV~\cite{johnson2023mimiciv}).
The overall B2$\rightarrow$B3 CI crosses zero (\ci{-1.5\pp}{+5.9\pp}).
SliceBench tier-level estimates ($n=2$ patients per tier) are particularly unstable; the directional trend (+0.6 to +5.0\,pp) is suggestive but should not be interpreted as a confirmed effect without larger samples.
Bootstrap CIs are computed at the question level; a cluster-aware bootstrap at the patient level would be more conservative and is planned for the expanded cohort.
Calculator/guideline integration (C5) showed degradation on ClinicalBench ($68.2\%$ vs.\ C4's $71.5\%$) that we hypothesize results from non-relevant module activation, though we have not performed controlled ablations to confirm this mechanism; query-aware component selection is a priority for future work.
SliceBench's LLM-as-judge evaluation may introduce biases; a pilot human validation ($n=5$, single reviewer; Appendix~\ref{app:human}) revealed overly strict automated scoring on uncertainty and sequence categories.
ClinicalBench uses a deterministic keyword evaluator to avoid this, but keyword matching may miss semantically correct answers that use different phrasing.
A full two-physician blinded adjudication covering both benchmarks is planned but not yet complete.
As noted in Section~\ref{sec:results}, run-to-run variance in MedGemma results ($\pm 10\pp$) means all reported deltas rely on within-run paired comparisons; cross-run comparisons are directional only.
C4g's aggregate accuracy ($-5.5\pp$ vs.\ C1) illustrates a key tension: structured context can \emph{hurt} in the aggregate even while providing transformative category-level gains ($+60\pp$ on change, $+57\pp$ on family history), because formatting overhead degrades categories where the LLM already performs well.
More broadly, systems like \epikg could improve clinical decision support by surfacing cross-admission patterns that clinicians may miss under time pressure; however, over-reliance on automated KG-driven summaries risks anchoring bias if the underlying NLP extraction or assertion classification introduces systematic errors, and evaluation on a single US academic medical center (MIMIC-IV) leaves generalization to other settings unvalidated (Appendix~\ref{app:ethics}).

\paragraph{Future work.}
Four directions follow:
(1)~Model-adaptive evidence presentation to address regression on strong-baseline categories (conditional $-35.0\pp$, duration~$-53.3\pp$ under C4g).
(2)~Improving evidence formatting for historical questions, where C4g degrades from 94\% (C1) to 40\% despite correct retrieval coverage---the 60\% change-detection ceiling similarly suggests formatting, not retrieval, as the bottleneck.
(3)~Expanding Tier~C to 20+ patients for sufficient statistical power.
(4)~Two-physician blinded adjudication on a stratified hard subset.

\paragraph{Conclusion.}
The epistemic propagation gap---the systematic loss of assertion, temporality, and experiencer metadata between NLP extraction and downstream reasoning---is a real and measurable phenomenon: the information-theoretic bound (Proposition~\ref{prop:entropy}) shows that collapsing non-present assertions to present destroys recoverable clinical information, and our ClinicalBench results confirm the empirical consequence---assertion-blind retrieval (C2) degrades accuracy by $9.0\pp$ while assertion-aware retrieval (C4) preserves it.
\epikg addresses this gap through end-to-end epistemic preservation combined with intent-aware retrieval that matches graph traversal strategy to question semantics.
The resulting benefit is category-specific and transformative rather than uniformly positive: change questions improve from 0\% to 60\% and family history from 0\% to 57\% under C4g, while categories where the LLM's parametric knowledge already suffices can regress.
This category$\times$condition interaction---not aggregate accuracy---is the right lens for evaluating clinical KG-RAG systems, and we argue that future benchmarks should report category-stratified effects as the primary outcome.
