\paragraph{Why long context underperforms.}
Brute-force long context (C6) scores 40.2\%, outperforming C1 ($+17.8\pp$) because it at least provides clinical text for the model to draw on, but it still falls far short of C4g (69.0\%) and catastrophically fails on historical (0\%) and sequence (0\%).
C6 concatenates all notes chronologically without retrieval or selection; more sophisticated long-context strategies (hierarchical summarization, temporal segmentation) might perform better, but C6's category-level failures---consistent with the ``lost in the middle'' phenomenon~\cite{liu2024lostmiddle}---demonstrate that raw context dumping cannot substitute for structured retrieval.
Historical questions require distinguishing resolved from active conditions across admissions; sequence questions require strict temporal ordering---both are implicit in raw text but explicit in the epistemic KG.
C6 handles change detection reasonably (40\%) because the relevant information appears explicitly in the text, but KG-RAG achieves $100\%$ ($+60\pp$).

\paragraph{Model-dependent KG benefit.}
The most striking finding is that KG-RAG benefit is inversely related to parametric medical knowledge (Table~\ref{tab:crossmodel}).
Opus, a general-purpose model, gains $+47\pp$ from intent-aware KG-RAG; GPT-OSS~20B gains $+39\pp$; MedGemma~27B, fine-tuned on medical corpora, gains $+32\pp$.
All three models benefit substantially, but the magnitude differs: MedGemma starts with the highest C1 baseline (26.5\%) yet achieves the lowest C4g accuracy (58.1\%), while Opus starts at 22.5\% but reaches 69.0\%.
Intent-aware KG-RAG thus functions as a \emph{medical knowledge compensator}: the structured epistemic context partially substitutes for parametric medical knowledge, providing the largest benefit to models that need it most.
This has practical implications: the same RAG system may help or hurt differently depending on the downstream model, and evaluation on a single model understates or overstates the system's value.

\paragraph{When KG context matters.}
The complexity-dependent SliceBench effect (Tier~C: $+5.0\pp$ vs.\ Tier~A: $+0.6\pp$) suggests structured epistemic context is most valuable when: (a)~document volume overwhelms chunk-level retrieval, (b)~questions require cross-encounter synthesis where the same concept appears with different assertion states, and (c)~assertion status evolves across encounters.
C4g's intent-aware routing provides direct evidence: the ``change'' category jumps from 17\% (C1) to 100\% ($+83\pp$), confirming that \emph{targeted graph traversal strategy}, not text access, is the key factor.

\paragraph{Uncertainty remains weakest.}
Uncertainty improves from 12.5\% to 50.0\% ($+37.5\pp$), but absolute accuracy remains low.
Possible and suspected conditions are linguistically subtle (``consider statin,'' ``may have CHF'') and require pragmatic inference beyond keyword matching.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
\begin{sloppypar}
Sample size is modest: ClinicalBench covers 45 patients (400 questions) and SliceBench 6 patients (144 questions), both from MIMIC-IV~\cite{johnson2023mimiciv}.
The B2$\rightarrow$B3 CI crosses zero (\ci{-1.5\pp}{+5.9\pp}); tier-level estimates ($n\!=\!2$ patients per tier) are hypothesis-generating only.
Model-dependent effects mean headline gains ($+47\pp$) are specific to Opus~+~C4g; MedGemma shows $+32\pp$ with smaller absolute improvement.
The keyword evaluator may miss semantically correct answers; physician adjudication (Section~\ref{sec:results:physician}) will address this.
This is an ablation study; comparisons with medical RAG systems~\cite{luo2025gfmrag,jiang2025kare,lu2025doctorrag} were not performed because these target factoid QA rather than patient-level longitudinal reasoning.
Bootstrap CIs are question-level; patient-level clustering (accounting for within-patient correlation) is planned.
MedGemma run-to-run variance ($\pm 10\pp$) arises from GPU non-determinism in 4-bit GGUF (GPT-Generated Unified Format) inference.
Over-reliance on KG-driven summaries risks anchoring bias if NLP extraction introduces systematic errors; single-site evaluation (US academic center) leaves generalization unvalidated (Appendix~\ref{app:ethics}).
\end{sloppypar}

\paragraph{Conclusion.}
The epistemic propagation gap is a real and measurable phenomenon: without retrieval, models achieve only 23\% on assertion-sensitive questions, while intent-aware epistemic KG-RAG lifts accuracy to 69\% ($+47\pp$).
\epikg addresses this gap through end-to-end epistemic preservation combined with intent-aware retrieval that matches graph traversal strategy to question semantics.
The benefit is category-specific: change detection rises from 17\% to 100\% ($+83\pp$), sequence from 10\% to 65\% ($+55\pp$), historical from 6\% to 60\% ($+54\pp$)---all 9 categories improve.
The benefit is also model-dependent: KG-RAG functions as a medical knowledge compensator across all three models tested, providing the largest gains to general-purpose models.
Neither more context nor structured data alone suffices---C6 (40.2\%) and C7 (3.8\%) bracket the design space---confirming that the category$\times$condition interaction, not the aggregate, is the right lens for clinical KG-RAG evaluation.
