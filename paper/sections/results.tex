We evaluate \epikg through two benchmarks: ClinicalBench tests assertion-sensitive and longitudinal reasoning, while SliceBench measures longitudinal QA across patient complexity tiers.
All confidence intervals use the bias-corrected and accelerated (BCa) bootstrap ($n=2{,}000$, seed 42)~\cite{efron1993bootstrap}, resampled at the question level.
ClinicalBench results use a deterministic keyword evaluator for reproducibility; SliceBench uses LLM-as-judge evaluation (Section~\ref{sec:bench:protocol}).

\paragraph{Methodological note on variance.}
MedGemma 27B runs via Ollama showed measurable run-to-run variance (C1 baseline fluctuated $\pm 10\pp$ across runs), attributable to evaluator refinement during development (Appendix~\ref{app:reproducibility}) and residual model non-determinism.
All ClinicalBench results reported in this section are \emph{within-run paired comparisons}: conditions were evaluated in the same run with the same evaluator version and model state, ensuring that deltas between conditions are internally valid even when absolute levels vary across runs.
Cross-run comparisons (e.g., between MedGemma and Opus) should be treated as directional.

C4 & + Epistemic KG-RAG & \textbf{71.5\%} & \textbf{0.775} \\

Adding vanilla RAG \emph{hurts}: C2 underperforms C1 by $9.0\pp$ on accuracy and $0.109$ on safety.
Retrieved passages like ``patient denies diabetes'' enter context without epistemic framing, leading the LLM to conflate mention with presence---consistent with GraphRAG-Bench~\cite{graphragbench2026}.
Assertion-blind KG-RAG (C3) compounds the problem, scoring lowest (59.2\%), as structured paths presenting negated conditions as graph edges actively mislead the model.
Epistemic KG-RAG (C4) recovers to 71.5\% and achieves the highest safety score (0.775 vs.\ 0.752 for C1), matching C1's accuracy while producing less clinically dangerous errors on average.
Intent-aware KG-RAG (C4g) scores 66.0\% overall---$5.5\pp$ below C1---but as we show below, this aggregate masks the condition's transformative effect on longitudinal question categories.

Table~\ref{tab:clinicalbench_categories} and Figures~\ref{fig:radar}--\ref{fig:deltas} reveal that the central finding is not ``system X beats system Y'' but a \emph{category$\times$condition interaction}: structured epistemic context is transformative for questions requiring cross-admission normalization but can hurt where the LLM's parametric knowledge already suffices.

\paragraph{Where intent-aware routing excels.}
The ``change'' category---near zero for \emph{all} conditions C1 through C5 (0.0--3.3\%)---jumps to 60.0\% under C4g ($+60.0\pp$ vs.\ C1, Figure~\ref{fig:deltas}).
This is the paper's central result: change questions require comparing a clinical concept across two admissions, which is impossible from a single note (C1), missed by chunk-level retrieval (C2), and lost in assertion-unaware graph paths (C3).
Even C4's epistemic metadata is insufficient because it retrieves paths without partitioning by admission; C4g's intent classifier detects question type and routes to admission-partitioned retrieval.
Current state also benefits ($+12.0\pp$), as does sequence ($+10.0\pp$), both requiring temporal awareness.
Family history rises from 0.0\% to 56.7\%, requiring experiencer attribution that only the graph-backed conditions provide.

\paragraph{Where intent-aware routing hurts.}
C4g loses substantially on historical ($-54.0\pp$), duration ($-53.3\pp$), and conditional ($-35.0\pp$).
For these categories, C1's parametric knowledge was already at or near ceiling (94.0\%, 100.0\%, 100.0\% respectively), and the injected graph context overrides correct reasoning---a known failure mode of RAG systems~\cite{graphragbench2026}.

\paragraph{Hard longitudinal subset.}
On the hard longitudinal subset defined in Section~\ref{sec:bench:clinicalbench} ($n=130$), C4g achieves 58.5\%, compared to C1 at 60.8\%, C2 at 50.8\%, C3 at 46.2\%, C4 at 44.6\%, and C5 at 49.2\%.
The C4$\rightarrow$C4g transition ($+13.8\pp$) demonstrates that intent-aware routing is the critical mechanism for longitudinal reasoning, even though C4g does not dominate on aggregate.

The magnitude of category-level effects depends on baseline model strength, but the pattern---gains on assertion-dependent categories, losses where parametric knowledge suffices---is consistent.

\subsection{Supporting Results}
\label{sec:results:supporting}

On MedQA-USMLE (965 questions), Claude Opus 4.5 achieves 81.6\% accuracy (5.1\,pp below GPT-4 at 86.7\% and Med-PaLM~2 at 86.5\%~\cite{medagentbench2025}), establishing that the Claude model family---Sonnet 4.5 (SliceBench answerer) and Opus 4.6 (ClinicalBench cross-model, SliceBench judge)---provides a reasonable LLM baseline.
Graph traversal latency is sub-millisecond at current scale (3,100 nodes, 8,803 edges): 0.57\,ms (1-hop), 0.75\,ms (2-hop).
The full system integrates 201 calculators, 1,202 guideline sections, 101 trigger patterns, and 24 edge types across 13 node types.
