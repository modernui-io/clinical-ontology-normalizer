% Section 5: Results
% NOTE: \section{Results} and \label{sec:results} are in main.tex

We evaluate \epikg through two benchmarks: ClinicalBench tests assertion-sensitive and longitudinal reasoning, while SliceBench measures longitudinal QA across patient complexity tiers.
ClinicalBench uses a deterministic keyword evaluator for reproducibility; SliceBench uses LLM-as-judge evaluation (Section~\ref{sec:bench:protocol}).
Claude Opus 4.6 is the primary answering model; MedGemma 27B provides cross-model validation.
Both benchmarks report bias-corrected and accelerated (BCa) bootstrap 95\% CIs ($n=2{,}000$, seed 42)~\cite{efron1993bootstrap}, resampled at the question level.

\subsection{ClinicalBench: Primary Ablation}
\label{sec:results:clinicalbench}

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{ClinicalBench primary results (400 questions, Claude Opus 4.6, keyword evaluator). C7 is model-independent (no LLM); remaining conditions use Opus 4.6. Sig: * denotes BCa CI excluding zero. Best in \textbf{bold}.}
\label{tab:clinicalbench_ablation}
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}llcl@{}}
\toprule
& Condition & Accuracy & $\Delta$ vs C1 \\
\midrule
C7 & Deterministic KG (no LLM) & 3.8\% & $-45.7\pp$ \\
\midrule
C1 & LLM Alone & 49.5\% & --- \\
C4 & + Epistemic KG-RAG & 60.8\% & $+11.3\pp$ \\
C4g & + Intent-Aware KG-RAG & \textbf{77.0\%} & $+27.5\pp$ * \\
\midrule
C6 & Long Context (all notes) & 41.8\% & $-7.7\pp$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The bookend baselines bracket the design space: C7 (deterministic KG, no LLM) achieves 3.8\%---only negation detection works (13.6\%) via stored assertion metadata---confirming that an LLM is essential for reasoning over graph evidence.
C6 (all notes concatenated) scores 41.8\%, \emph{worse} than C1 ($-7.7\pp$): brute-force long context is not a viable alternative, even with Opus's 200K token window.
Uniform epistemic KG-RAG (C4) achieves 60.8\% ($+11.3\pp$), showing that structured retrieval with assertion metadata provides benefit even without intent-aware routing.
Intent-aware KG-RAG (C4g) adds another $+16.2\pp$ to reach 77.0\% ($+27.5\pp$ vs.\ C1; 95\% BCa CI: \ci{+21.7\pp}{+32.5\pp})---the largest single-step improvement in the ablation, confirming that question-type-specific traversal is critical.
The C4$\rightarrow$C4g transition is most visible on change detection (23\%$\rightarrow$100\%) and current state (32\%$\rightarrow$72\%), where uniform retrieval injects irrelevant temporal context that intent-aware routing avoids.

Table~\ref{tab:clinicalbench_categories} and Figure~\ref{fig:radar} reveal a strong \emph{category$\times$condition interaction}: intent-aware KG-RAG improves 8 of 9 categories with zero regressions---sequence is already at 100\% under C1 because the keyword evaluator matches temporal ordering terms that Opus produces from parametric knowledge alone (C6 drops to 0\% because the raw notes bury ordering cues).

\paragraph{Where intent-aware routing excels.}
\begin{sloppypar}
The largest gains occur on categories requiring cross-admission synthesis: change ($+90\pp$), historical ($+56\pp$), family history ($+53\pp$), uncertainty ($+32.5\pp$), and current state ($+32\pp$).
On the hard longitudinal subset (change + current state + historical, $n=130$), C4g outperforms C1 by $+54.6\pp$ (95\% BCa CI: \ci{+43.8\pp}{+63.8\pp}).
Five of these seven improvements exceed $+30\pp$; the remaining two---negation ($+1.8\pp$, $n=110$) and duration ($+6.7\pp$, $n=30$)---are small relative to their per-category sampling variability and should be interpreted as noise-level (Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:deltas}).
C6 fails catastrophically on historical (0\%) and sequence (0\%) despite Opus's 200K window, confirming that long context cannot substitute for structured retrieval.
\end{sloppypar}

\subsection{Cross-Model Validation}
\label{sec:results:crossmodel}

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{ClinicalBench cross-model results (400 questions, keyword evaluator). KG-RAG benefit is inversely related to parametric medical knowledge.}
\label{tab:crossmodel}
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}llccl@{}}
\toprule
& Model & C1 & C4g & $\Delta$ \\
\midrule
Commercial & Claude Opus 4.6 & 49.5\% & \textbf{77.0\%} & $+27.5\pp$ \\
Open & MedGemma 27B & 52.5\% & 66.2\% & $+13.7\pp$ \\
% Qwen 35B row removed — experiment not yet complete
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The KG-RAG benefit is model-dependent and inversely related to parametric medical knowledge (Table~\ref{tab:crossmodel}).
Both models benefit from KG-RAG, but the magnitude differs: Opus gains $+27.5\pp$ while MedGemma gains $+13.7\pp$.

This pattern is most visible on historical questions: MedGemma C4g achieves 46\% on historical (vs.\ 0\% for C1), while Opus C4g reaches 62\% (vs.\ 6\% for C1).
The general-purpose model gains more from structured retrieval because it lacks the parametric medical knowledge that MedGemma acquired through fine-tuning.
Intent-aware KG-RAG thus functions as a \emph{medical knowledge compensator}: the structured epistemic context partially substitutes for parametric medical knowledge, providing the largest benefit to models that need it most.
The benefit is not uniform across categories: MedGemma regresses on negation ($-17.2\pp$), conditional ($-10\pp$), and duration ($-6.7\pp$) under C4g, where structured retrieval overrides correct parametric answers (per-category cross-model breakdowns in Appendix Table~\ref{tab:crossmodel_categories}).
