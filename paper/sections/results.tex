We evaluate \epikg through two benchmarks: ClinicalBench tests assertion-sensitive reasoning, while Slice Bench measures longitudinal QA across patient complexity tiers.
All confidence intervals use the bias-corrected and accelerated (BCa) bootstrap ($n=2{,}000$, seed 42)~\cite{efron1993bootstrap}, resampled at the question level.

\subsection{ClinicalBench: Assertion-Sensitive Reasoning}
\label{sec:results:clinicalbench}

Table~\ref{tab:clinicalbench_ablation} presents the main ablation results across five conditions (MedGemma 27B, LLM-as-judge).

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{ClinicalBench ablation results (400 questions, MedGemma 27B). Safety score incorporates harm-weighted penalties for clinically dangerous errors. Best per column in \textbf{bold}.}
\label{tab:clinicalbench_ablation}
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}llcc@{}}
\toprule
& Condition & Accuracy & Safety \\
\midrule
C1 & LLM Alone & 71.5\% & 0.752 \\
C2 & + Vanilla RAG & 62.5\% & 0.643 \\
C3 & + KG-RAG (no assertion) & 59.3\% & 0.562 \\
C4 & + Epistemic KG-RAG & \textbf{71.5\%} & \textbf{0.775} \\
C5 & + Full System & 68.3\% & 0.734 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Adding vanilla RAG \emph{hurts}: C2 underperforms C1 by $9.0\pp$ on accuracy and $0.109$ on safety.
Retrieved passages like ``patient denies diabetes'' enter context without epistemic framing, leading the LLM to conflate mention with presence---consistent with GraphRAG-Bench~\cite{graphragbench2026}.
Assertion-blind KG-RAG (C3) compounds the problem, scoring lowest (59.3\%, 0.562), as structured paths presenting negated conditions as graph edges actively mislead the model.
Epistemic KG-RAG (C4) matches C1 at 71.5\% aggregate accuracy but with a fundamentally different category profile (Table~\ref{tab:clinicalbench_categories}), and achieves a higher safety score (0.775 vs.\ 0.752), indicating that C4's errors are less clinically dangerous on average.
C5 drops $3.2\pp$ below C4; we hypothesize this results from calculator and guideline modules activating on queries not requiring them, though controlled per-component ablations are needed to confirm this (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

\begin{figure}[t]

\caption{ClinicalBench per-category accuracy (Change omitted; near zero for all). C4 (solid blue) gains on uncertainty and family history but loses on historical, conditional, and duration vs.\ C1 (dotted gray). C2 (dashed red) degrades broadly.}

Table~\ref{tab:clinicalbench_categories} and Figure~\ref{fig:radar} reveal that C4's matching aggregate masks large bidirectional category shifts.
C4 gains on categories requiring structured epistemic metadata: $+70.0\pp$ on family history (0.0\%$\to$70.0\%), $+20.0\pp$ on uncertainty (37.5\%$\to$57.5\%), and $+7.5\pp$ on sequence.
However, C4 loses substantially on categories where C1's parametric knowledge was already strong: historical ($-34.0\pp$, from 94.0\% to 60.0\%), conditional ($-30.0\pp$), and duration ($-16.7\pp$).
The net effect is an accuracy profile trade-off, not a uniform improvement.
The higher safety score (0.775 vs.\ 0.752) reflects that C4's category gains involve high-risk assertion errors (e.g., misattributing a family member's condition to the patient), while its losses occur on categories with lower clinical harm potential.
The ``change'' category remains near zero across all conditions (0.0--3.3\%), a genuine capability gap.

With only 2 patients per tier these are point estimates without per-tier CIs, so the pattern is suggestive rather than confirmed.
If replicated, it would be consistent with the hypothesis that structured KG context is most valuable when document volume overwhelms chunk-level retrieval.

\subsection{Cross-Model Validation}
\label{sec:results:crossmodel}

To test whether epistemic KG-RAG generalizes beyond a single model, we ran ClinicalBench with Claude Opus 4.6 (commercial, 200B+ parameter class) alongside MedGemma 27B (open-source, on-device).
Both use a deterministic keyword evaluator for reproducibility (Appendix~\ref{app:reproducibility}).

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{ClinicalBench cross-model results (400 questions, keyword evaluator). Within-run paired comparisons control for evaluator version and model state.}
\label{tab:crossmodel}
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}llccc@{}}
\toprule
& Model & C1 (LLM Alone) & C4 (Epistemic KG-RAG) & $\Delta$ \\
\midrule
Open & MedGemma 27B & 58.0\% & 67.0\% & $+9.0\pp$ \\
Commercial & Claude Opus 4.6 & 72.5\% & 70.2\% & $-2.2\pp$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Table~\ref{tab:crossmodel} reveals a model-strength interaction: epistemic KG-RAG produces a large gain for the weaker model ($+9.0\pp$) but a small regression for the stronger one ($-2.2\pp$).
Table~\ref{tab:crossmodel_categories} shows the category-level mechanism: both models gain on negation, duration, and uncertainty, where structured assertion metadata provides information absent from the LLM's parametric knowledge.
However, Opus loses on categories where it was already strong---conditional ($-15.0\pp$), family history ($-10.0\pp$), current state ($-10.0\pp$)---suggesting the evidence overrides correct parametric reasoning in these cases.

This pattern---large gains for weaker models, small regressions for stronger ones---suggests that epistemic evidence is most valuable when the LLM lacks sufficient parametric knowledge to reason about assertion status independently.
For stronger models, the current evidence format may override correct reasoning; model-adaptive evidence presentation (e.g., lighter context for high-capability models) is a promising direction (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

Slice Bench (Claude Sonnet 4.5) and ClinicalBench (MedGemma 27B, Claude Opus 4.6) show directionally consistent findings across three model families: vanilla RAG can degrade assertion-sensitive performance, while epistemic KG-RAG recovers or improves accuracy, with the magnitude depending on baseline model strength.

\subsection{Supporting Results}
\label{sec:results:supporting}

On MedQA-USMLE (965 questions), the backbone LLM achieves 81.6\% accuracy (5.1\,pp below GPT-4 at 86.7\% and Med-PaLM~2 at 86.5\%~\cite{medagentbench2025}), establishing a reasonable baseline for the system's underlying language model.
Graph traversal latency is sub-millisecond at current scale (3,100 nodes, 8,803 edges): 0.57\,ms (1-hop), 0.75\,ms (2-hop).
The full system integrates 201 calculators, 1,202 guideline sections, 101 trigger patterns, and 24 edge types across 13 node types.
