Clinical NLP pipelines detect assertion status---negation, uncertainty, family history---with $F_1 > 0.90$, yet no existing system propagates these labels through knowledge graph construction into retrieval-augmented generation.
We formalize this \emph{epistemic propagation gap} as information loss: when assertion labels are discarded during graph construction, the Shannon entropy of the epistemic state collapses to zero, upper-bounding the faithfulness of any downstream generation that conditions on the graph.
We present \epikg, a clinical knowledge graph that closes this gap with a 7-value assertion schema carried on every edge, a tri-temporal model (document, event, and ingestion time) governed by Allen's interval algebra, and an intent-aware retrieval layer that dispatches change, current-state, and historical questions to specialized graph-traversal strategies.
On the hard longitudinal subset of ClinicalBench (change, current-state, historical; $n=130$, MIMIC-IV), intent-aware epistemic KG-RAG improves over LLM-alone by $[{+X\,\text{pp}}]$, while vanilla RAG \emph{degrades} overall accuracy by $9.0\pp$---assertion-blind retrieval is strictly worse than no retrieval.
Cross-model validation reveals a model-strength interaction: structured epistemic context yields $+9.0\pp$ for MedGemma~27B but $-2.2\pp$ for Claude Opus~4.6, indicating that the benefit concentrates where the LLM lacks sufficient parametric clinical knowledge.
On SliceBench, KG gains scale with patient complexity ($+5.0\pp$ for patients with $\geq 15$ encounters vs.\ $+0.6\pp$ for 1--2), confirming that structured epistemic context is most valuable precisely when longitudinal complexity overwhelms simple retrieval and parametric knowledge alone.
