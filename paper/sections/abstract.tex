Clinical NLP extracts assertion status (negated, uncertain, family-history, etc.), but these labels are often dropped before retrieval, causing downstream QA to treat epistemically distinct facts as equivalent.
We call this failure mode the \emph{epistemic propagation gap}.
We introduce \epikg, a clinical KG-RAG pipeline that preserves a 7-value assertion schema and tri-temporal metadata on graph edges, then uses intent-aware routing to select question-type-specific traversals.

On ClinicalBench (400 questions, 43 MIMIC-IV patients; Claude Opus 4.6), intent-aware KG-RAG (C4g) reaches 69.0\% versus 21.8\% for LLM-alone ($+47.2\pp$).
Ablation isolates the mechanism: structured retrieval contributes $+28.2\pp$ (C1$\to$C3), adding assertions without routing hurts ($-3.2\pp$, C3$\to$C4), and intent-aware routing recovers and amplifies gains ($+22.2\pp$, C4$\to$C4g).
Thus, assertion metadata is necessary but not sufficient.
Long-context concatenation (C6) reaches 39.0\% but fails on historical and sequence (0\% each), showing that context scale alone does not solve longitudinal reasoning.

Cross-model validation (Opus 4.6, GPT-OSS 20B, MedGemma 27B) shows consistent C1$\to$C4g gains ($+31.5$ to $+47.2\pp$), with model-dependent magnitude.
In a blinded physician pilot ($n=30$), C4g answers were rated more correct (81\% vs.\ 29\%), safer (88\% vs.\ 50\%), and more helpful (81\% vs.\ 29\%).
Physician review also found 40\% errors in auto-generated gold labels, motivating claim calibration and multi-evaluator reporting.
Overall, we show that category$\times$condition interactions, rather than aggregate accuracy alone, are the critical lens for evaluating clinical KG-RAG.
