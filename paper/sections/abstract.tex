Clinical NLP pipelines detect assertion status---negation, uncertainty, family history---with $F_1 > 0.90$~\cite{gul2025beyondnegation}, yet no existing system propagates these labels through knowledge graph construction into retrieval-augmented generation.
We call this the \emph{epistemic propagation gap}: when assertion labels are discarded during graph construction, downstream generation cannot faithfully represent the epistemic state of clinical findings.
We present \epikg, a clinical knowledge graph that closes this gap with a 7-value assertion schema on every edge, a tri-temporal model (valid time, transaction time, and NLP-asserted temporality), and an intent-aware retrieval layer that dispatches question-type-specific graph traversals.
On ClinicalBench ($n=400$, MIMIC-IV, Claude Opus 4.6), intent-aware KG-RAG achieves 69\% overall ($+47\pp$ vs.\ LLM alone at 22\%), improving all 9 categories: change detection rises from 7\% to 100\% ($+93\pp$), sequence reasoning from 10\% to 65\% ($+55\pp$), and historical reasoning from 6\% to 60\% ($+54\pp$).
An ablation decomposition via C3 (KG-RAG without assertions) shows structured retrieval alone contributes $+28\pp$; epistemic annotations add a further $+19\pp$, with change detection rising from 17\% (C3) to 100\% (C4g).
Brute-force long context collapses to 0\% on historical and sequence categories despite Opus's 200K token window.
Cross-model validation on three models reveals that KG-RAG benefit is inversely related to parametric medical knowledge: MedGemma 27B gains $+32\pp$ vs.\ Opus's $+47\pp$, consistent with a knowledge-compensator effect.
GPT-OSS 20B gains $+38\pp$, further confirming the pattern.
A two-physician blinded adjudication is preregistered.
These results establish the category$\times$condition interaction---not the aggregate---as the right evaluation lens for clinical KG-RAG.
