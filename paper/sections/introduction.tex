A physician documents ``patient denies chest pain, sister had MI at 45, will consider statin if lipids remain elevated''---a single sentence encoding four epistemic states: a negated symptom, a family-history event, a hypothetical action, and an implicit present condition.
Clinical NLP can detect these assertions accurately~\cite{uzuner2011i2b2assertion, gul2025beyondnegation}, yet when a RAG system ingests the same note, assertion context is flattened: negation is lost, family attribution collapses onto the patient, and downstream reasoning may conclude the patient \emph{has} chest pain and \emph{had} an MI.
We call this the \emph{epistemic propagation gap}---the systematic loss of assertion status as clinical information moves from extraction through storage to retrieval.

The gap persists because, to our knowledge, prior KG-RAG systems do not propagate assertion labels beyond the extraction stage into graph-augmented retrieval.
Clinical NLP pipelines (cTAKES, John Snow Labs) detect assertions, but do not carry them as edge attributes into knowledge graph construction or retrieval.
OMOP (Observational Medical Outcomes Partnership) excludes negated conditions from \texttt{CONDITION\_OCCURRENCE}~\cite{omop2024}; FHIR (Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources) provides \texttt{verificationStatus} for \texttt{Condition} resources only, covering a fraction of the clinical data model.
Graph-augmented RAG systems---GraphRAG~\cite{edge2024graphrag}, GFM-RAG~\cite{luo2025gfmrag}, KARE~\cite{jiang2025kare}, Medical-Graph-RAG~\cite{wu2025medicalgraphrag}---build KGs that discard the metadata distinguishing ``patient has diabetes'' from ``rule out diabetes.''
Two KG surveys~\cite{alkhatib2024pckgsurvey,abusalih2023healthcarekg} do not identify assertion preservation as a concern.
A parallel \emph{temporal integration gap} exists: clinical events span three time dimensions (valid time, transaction time, NLP-asserted temporality), yet existing systems model at most two~\cite{huang2024medtkg,ramirez2025graphiti}.
Allen's interval algebra~\cite{allen1983temporal} has been formalized as annotation ontologies~\cite{li2020teo} and modal operators~\cite{ong2023tcl}, but never as first-class KG edge attributes that participate in retrieval.

We present \epikg, a clinical KG system that closes both gaps by preserving epistemic status end-to-end from NLP extraction through OMOP mapping, fact construction, KG materialization, and graph-augmented retrieval.
Our contributions:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, topsep=4pt, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Epistemic preservation invariant.} We formalize the requirement that assertion status remain recoverable across extraction, mapping, storage, and retrieval; derive an information-theoretic loss bound for assertion-blind pipelines (Section~\ref{sec:system:formal}, Appendix~\ref{app:formal}); and implement a seven-value assertion schema carried end-to-end.

  \item \textbf{Tri-temporal epistemic KG.} We represent valid time, transaction time, and NLP-asserted temporality jointly on edges, with Allen-style temporal relations as first-class retrieval features, enabling longitudinal reasoning over multi-admission records.

  \item \textbf{Ablation design that isolates mechanism.} We introduce C4 (assertions without intent routing) between C3 and C4g, and include C6/C7 as bookend baselines. This separates the effect of structured retrieval, assertion metadata, and routing policy instead of reporting a single bundled gain.

  \item \textbf{Multi-evaluator and cross-model validation.} We evaluate on two benchmarks (ClinicalBench, SliceBench), three models, and three evaluator views (deterministic keyword, LLM-as-judge, blinded physician pilot), reporting where estimates agree, where they diverge, and which conclusions are robust to evaluator choice.

  \item \textbf{A falsifiable empirical claim.} The key claim is not that ``KG-RAG is always better,'' but that epistemic metadata improves QA \emph{only when retrieval uses it}. Empirically, assertions without routing can hurt (C3$\to$C4), while routing unlocks gains (C4$\to$C4g), especially on hard longitudinal categories.
\end{enumerate}

Our central research question is: \emph{under what conditions does structured epistemic context help, hurt, or break even relative to unstructured retrieval and no retrieval?}
The answer is interactional rather than uniform: gains depend on question type, routing policy, and model.
Across experiments, intent-aware KG-RAG improves all nine ClinicalBench categories, with largest effects on cross-admission longitudinal tasks; assertions without routing can degrade temporal categories; and brute-force long context does not recover these failures despite a large token window.
These results motivate a narrower and more testable takeaway: category$\times$condition interactions are the appropriate evaluation target for clinical KG-RAG, while aggregate accuracy alone obscures mechanism (Section~\ref{sec:results}).
